Type of Volcano

A caldera is a large volcanic crater, typically formed by the collapse of a volcano into itself.
Also known as a glaciovolcan, they form beneath a glacier or ice sheet. They can have very explosive erruptions because of this.
A shield volcano is a type of volcano characterized by gentle slopes and a broad, shield-like shape, hence the name. These volcanoes are built by the eruption of low-viscosity basaltic lava flows, which tend to travel long distances before solidifying due to their fluid nature.
A rift zone is a region of Earth's crust where tectonic plates are being pulled apart, leading to the formation of a linear series of faults, fractures, and volcanic activity.

Stratovolcanoes are typically formed at convergent plate boundaries, where one tectonic plate is forced beneath another in a process known as subduction.
